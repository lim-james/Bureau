# Founding Team

## Purpose

The founding team is the bureau's first act. Before any operational team is formed, before any build begins, the founding team is assembled to turn a task into a structured, actionable direction for the bureau.

## Responsibilities

- Discuss and clarify the scope of the project
- Surface and resolve ambiguity in the task description — task descriptions are not treated as gospel
- Perform research where necessary to fill gaps in understanding
- Produce a **direction contract** (see below) that all subsequent teams are oriented toward
- Drive development forward through critique and ideas throughout the project's lifetime

## Composition

- Founding members are appointed, not self-selected
- Roles within the founding team lean non-technical at first — research, strategy, design — as no build is happening yet
- Technical members may be included where their expertise is needed to define scope accurately
- Founding members may later be appointed as partners

## Lifecycle

The founding team is not automatically dissolved after the direction contract is set. The bureau may choose one of three paths:

1. **Retain as oversight** — the founding team remains intact as a visionary body for the duration of the project, driving development forward with critique and ideas
2. **Partial reassignment** — some founding members are reassigned to operational teams where their knowledge of the original vision is most needed
3. **Full dissolution** — all founding members are reassigned; the vision lives in the contract, not the team

The bureau makes this call. Founding members do not self-determine their own continuation.

**The mission guardian must survive dissolution.** The founding team is the default holder of intent-authority ("escalate to founding" for direction-level matters — see [External Feedback](./feedback.md)). But if the founding team is fully dissolved, that authority must **transfer explicitly** to a surviving mission guardian (a retained seat or the human) — "escalate to founding" may never point at a body that no longer exists (see [Foundation](./foundation.md) §3). The guardian owns not only the mission's durability but the recurring question of its **validity** — *"is this mission still good, safe, and worth pursuing?"* — with the standing to halt and escalate to the human.

## Behavioural Expectations

- Founding members are **visionaries**, not reviewers. Their role is to drive progression, not to approve or rubber-stamp.
- If retained as an oversight body, they are expected to actively critique, challenge, and contribute ideas — not simply observe.
- Dissolution or reassignment of their own team is a decision they participate in objectively.

## The Direction Contract

The founding team's primary output is a direction contract — a shared understanding of:

- **What** the bureau is building and **why**
- The intent and goals that all teams are bound to serve

The contract defines **intent**, not **implementation**. Teams are bound to the spirit of the contract — the what and the why — but retain full freedom over the how. Individual innovation within teams is permitted and encouraged, as long as it serves the progression of the bureau.

**Falsifiable success criteria are mandatory.** The direction contract and every milestone must state success as **observable conditions written before work begins** — pre-registered pass/fail, not "make it better." Without this an outcome can always be retro-narrated as success (an unfalsifiable claim). The contract sits *below* the constitution and the mission in the [supremacy order](./foundation.md) — it binds intent within them and proposes, never enacts, constitutional change.

### Conflict Resolution

If a team's direction conflicts with the founding contract, the matter is raised at a partner meeting. Partners evaluate whether the innovation serves the bureau's progression. If it does, the direction may be absorbed or acknowledged. If it does not, the original direction holds.
